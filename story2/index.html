Residents Voice Opposition to 5G

Will Campbell

Oct. 23, 2017

cell antennas




ROCKVILLE, Md. - Montgomery County residents pressured the county executive Monday to challenge the Federal Communication Commission’s regulation of small cell antenna service.

The majority of residents who spoke at the community meeting opposed the county’s zoning proposal to deploy small cell antennas that cell phone carriers require to provide fifth generation technology.

The county’s suggested solution for the small cell antennas involve changing zone setback and height restrictions relative to pole and antenna location within the county.

Annette Nelson, county resident, asked how the residents could stop the antenna deployment and Mitsuko Herrera, the director of the county’s Ultra Montgomery Program said, “...we want to have that service.”

Significant evidence links health issues, like cancer, to low-level radio frequency emissions said Bob Sonawane, a retired Environmental Protection Agency scientist.

“We need to wake up and protect the children as well as protect the citizens of the Montgomery County,” Sonawane stated.

Gerald Lederer, of Best, Best & Krieger and also the county’s legal counsel, said federal laws prohibit the county from denying wireless service because of radio frequency emissions.

A Montgomery County delegation has met with FCC multiple times requesting them to complete their study of health effects and to update radio frequency standards.

Two county residents, Donna Berin and Mario Pascel, expressed concern about the cumulative health effects of radio frequency emission and the proximity of antennas to people.

Herrera said no studies of cumulative health effects exist right now.

Andrew Afflerbach, chief executive officer of CTC Energy & Technology and the county’s engineering consultant, stated the height restrictions and locations in the county’s proposal have been picked and vetted because of radiation concerns.

County officials formed a task-force who enforces FCC’s radio frequency standards for the county by reviewing cell phone antenna applications and making recommendations to the county.

Mark King, county resident, proposed the county require telecommunication companies to establish a fund that compensates residents who lost property value because of cell tower placement.

“I believe that the county has the responsibility to establish the permitting criteria for the installation of these small towers, then the county bears the responsibility to protect homeowners throughout the county,” said King.

Herrera stated loss in property value is not limited to one factor, rather multiple factors.

“Children are going to be sleeping near these towers where we don't have studies that really show whether it's safe or not, how is that risk going to be managed,” said Kerry Cunningham, county resident.

Herrera stated: “In these cases the the owner and the operator of the antenna is the one that has the liability.”

“There is absolutely nothing that we have heard here tonight compelling the county to act in any manner on this issue right now,” said Megan Montgomery, county resident.

Antenna applications have already been filed and the FCC set a 60, 90, and 150 day deadline for the county to process applications per federal law.

“13 states have passed laws that would take away local government authority with respect to the deployment of small cells in the rights-of-way,” said Lederer.

Nancy Wallace, co-chair of the Montgomery County Green Party, said that the commission is ignoring numerous health studies and requested that the county file a lawsuit against the commission.

Herrera stated county officials are finding a local strategy instead of a strategy by state officials.
